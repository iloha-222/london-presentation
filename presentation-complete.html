Understanding the Framework

The Three Sacreds

Sacred Object

A thangka, statue, or ritual implement—vehicles for study and realization

Sacred Person

The practitioner or community who engages with the object through practice

Sacred Place

The temple, altar, stupa, monastery—where objects and persons meet

When separated, the connection is severed.

Can we bring them back into relationship?

March 2025

A Question Beyond Borders

SINI participated in LunARC, sending sacred Buddhist art to the moon on a hard drive that will last for a billion years.

If we can think about preserving art for a billion years on the moon—doesn't that change the conversation about museum collections?

The Challenge

The Knowledge Gap

Traditional Monastic Education

• 15-25 years to become a geshe

• Master logic, epistemology, ethics

• Study ritual, meditation, tantric practices

• Centuries-refined holistic education

Museum Resources

• Rare manuscripts unavailable elsewhere

• Historical objects documenting evolution

• Research databases with provenance

• Conservation knowledge for preservation

But they rarely connect.

Our Unique Position

Sarnath International Nyingma Institute

Founded by Tarthang Tulku Rinpoche in 1978

Located in Sarnath, where the Buddha first taught the Dharma. For over 40 years, SINI has been dedicated to preserving Tibetan Buddhist culture and education through innovative programs and international collaboration.

Our Unique Position

Why SINI Can Bridge This Gap

Sacred Location

Sarnath—where the Buddha gave his first teaching. One of Buddhism's most sacred sites where major teachings happen.

Representing Diversity

Students from all four schools: Nyingma, Kagyu, Gelug, Sakya. From Tibet, Bhutan, Nepal, India, Mongolia.

Heritage Preservation

40+ years preserving Tibetan culture. Reproducing sacred art from museums and distributing back to monasteries.

The Connector

Connecting remote monasteries to international institutions. Hundreds of small monasteries need this bridge.

Small enough to be nimble.

Connected enough to create bridges.

Component 3

Sacred Sites Integration

The Concept

Display high-quality images of sacred art at sacred sites during major gatherings—reconnecting object, person, and place

In Practice

When communities gather at sacred places like Bodh Gaya or Sarnath, they can see and study objects from their own traditions that now reside in museums worldwide

Component 4

Cultural Protocols

The Goal

Respecting sacred protocols while meeting international conservation standards

Example: Swayambhunath

UNESCO World Heritage stupa: documented 30,000 pieces, respected sacred protocols, met conservation standards

The Result

You can have both: Scientific rigor and spiritual respect working together

Not counting how many objects were physically returned but asking:

The Invitation

The urgency is real: We have a limited window to build these bridges while the generation that fled Tibet in 1959 is still here to guide us.

Can we make these collections serve living education while honoring their sacred nature?

Bridge the knowledge gap?

Model collaborative futures?

I believe we can.
